## Core Philosophy

- **Engineering mindset applied to finance**: Lyn's background as an industrial engineer shapes her approach — she treats the economy as a system of inputs, outputs, feedback loops, and constraints rather than relying on narrative-driven analysis.
- **Macro-first, then micro**: Understand the broad fiscal and monetary environment before picking individual investments. The macro tide lifts or sinks most boats.
- **Follow the money supply**: The single most important variable for asset prices is the growth or contraction of broad money (M2) relative to real economic output.
- **Think in purchasing power, not nominal terms**: A portfolio that returns 8% in a 10% inflation environment is losing wealth. Always measure returns against the cost of real goods, energy, and housing.

## The Fiscal/Monetary Framework

This is Lyn's most foundational analytical lens:

### The Four Quadrants

| | **Tight Fiscal Policy** | **Loose Fiscal Policy** |
|---|---|---|
| **Tight Monetary Policy** | Deflationary / Deleveraging | Inflationary despite rate hikes (fiscal dominance) |
| **Loose Monetary Policy** | Disinflationary (2010s paradigm) | Highly inflationary (1940s, 2020s) |

### Key Insights

1. **Fiscal policy has become the dominant force** — When governments run massive deficits (>6% of GDP outside of recession), monetary tightening (rate hikes) becomes far less effective because the government is injecting more spending into the economy than the central bank can remove.
2. **Fiscal dominance vs. monetary dominance** — The 1980s-2010s were an era of monetary dominance (central banks led, fiscal policy followed). The 2020s shifted to fiscal dominance (deficits are so large they overwhelm monetary policy).
3. **Interest expense spiral** — When debt/GDP is very high, raising rates increases government interest expense, which widens the deficit, which is itself inflationary. Rate hikes become self-defeating beyond a certain debt threshold.

## Long-Term Debt Cycle Framework

### The ~80-Year Debt Supercycle

- Nations go through long-term debt cycles lasting roughly 50-100 years.
- **Early cycle**: Low debt, high growth, rising trust in institutions, currency strengthening.
- **Mid cycle**: Debt accumulates, financialization increases, wealth inequality grows, manufacturing hollows out.
- **Late cycle**: Debt/GDP exceeds ~100%, currency debasement becomes necessary, social/political tension rises, trust in institutions erodes.
- **Resolution**: Historically resolved through inflation, currency devaluation, debt restructuring, or some combination. Rarely through austerity alone.

### Where We Are Now (2020s)

- The US, Europe, Japan, and China are all in the late stages of their respective long-term debt cycles.
- The US debt/GDP exceeded 120%. Federal interest expense surpassed defense spending.
- The most likely resolution path is **financial repression**: keeping interest rates below the rate of inflation for an extended period, gradually eroding debt in real terms (similar to the 1940s-1950s).

## Money & Currency Framework

### From *Broken Money* (2023)

#### What is Money?

- Money is a **ledger technology** — a way of tracking who owes what to whom.
- Throughout history, money has taken three forms:
  - **Commodity money** (gold, silver, salt) — hard to produce, hard to transport
  - **Bank credit money** (paper notes, bank deposits) — easy to transport, easy to produce (debasable)
  - **Digital bearer money** (Bitcoin) — easy to transport, hard to produce

#### The Money Trilemma

Any monetary system can optimize for at most **two of three** properties:

1. **Hardness** (resistance to debasement/inflation)
2. **Portability** (easy to move across distances and borders)
3. **Scalability** (ability to handle many transactions cheaply)

- Gold: Hard + (somewhat) scalable, but not portable at scale
- Fiat: Portable + scalable, but not hard
- Bitcoin (base layer): Hard + portable, but not scalable (yet — Lightning Network extends this)

#### Currency Devaluation Patterns

- All fiat currencies lose value over time — the question is the rate.
- Currency devaluations tend to happen in **steps, not gradually** — long periods of apparent stability punctuated by sharp drops.
- The best leading indicator of currency devaluation: **broad money supply growth relative to real GDP growth**.

## Asset Valuation Frameworks

### Equities

- **Use the equity risk premium (ERP)** — compare earnings yield (inverse of P/E) to Treasury yields. When the gap narrows, stocks are less attractive.
- **Shiller CAPE ratio** — useful for long-term return expectations (10+ years) but poor for timing. A CAPE above 30 historically implies below-average forward returns.
- **Nominal vs. real returns** — In inflationary periods, stocks can rise nominally but fall in real terms (1970s example: S&P 500 was flat for a decade in nominal terms, down ~60% in real terms).
- **Revenue growth > earnings manipulation** — Revenue is harder to manipulate than earnings. Look at price-to-sales alongside price-to-earnings.

### Bonds

- **Bonds are a bet on inflation** — In an era of fiscal dominance, long-duration bonds carry significant risk because inflation may persistently exceed yields.
- **The 60/40 portfolio is structurally challenged** — The 60% stocks / 40% bonds portfolio worked in a 40-year disinflationary environment (1982-2022). In an inflationary regime, bonds fail to provide the hedge they once did.
- **Short-duration over long-duration** in inflationary environments — T-bills and short-term bonds preserve optionality; long bonds lock in potentially negative real yields.

### Commodities & Real Assets

- **Commodities are underinvested** — After a decade of underinvestment (2010s), commodity production capacity is insufficient relative to demand, especially given the energy transition.
- **Energy is the master resource** — All economic activity is ultimately a transformation of energy. Cheap energy = growth; expensive energy = stagflation.
- **Capex cycles matter** — Commodity producers went through massive capex cuts in 2015-2020. New supply takes 5-10 years to come online. This creates structural shortages.

## Portfolio Construction Principles

### The "Barbell" Approach

Lyn advocates a barbell strategy combining:

1. **Hard assets / inflation hedges** (one end):
   - Gold and silver (physical + miners)
   - Bitcoin (small but meaningful allocation)
   - Commodity producers (energy, copper, uranium)
   - Real estate (selectively)
   - TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities)

2. **Cash-flowing businesses / equities** (other end):
   - Companies with pricing power and real assets
   - Dividend growers (not just high yielders)
   - Emerging market equities (selectively, where valuations are cheap)
   - Value over growth in inflationary regimes

3. **Avoid the middle**:
   - Long-duration bonds
   - Highly speculative/unprofitable growth stocks
   - Assets that depend on continued low inflation

### Key Portfolio Principles

- **Diversify across asset types, not just within equities** — Most investors are over-concentrated in US large-cap equities and US dollar-denominated assets.
- **Geographic diversification matters** — The US has outperformed dramatically since 2010, but this is partly due to dollar strength and tech concentration. Reversion to the mean is likely.
- **Rebalance based on macro regime, not calendar** — Don't mechanically rebalance; shift allocations based on which quadrant of the fiscal/monetary framework you're in.
- **Liquidity is a position** — Holding cash (short-duration instruments) is not "doing nothing." It's optionality for buying dislocations.

## Sector & Asset Class Views

### Bitcoin

- **Bitcoin is a savings technology, not a payments technology** (at the base layer).
- It solves the portability problem that gold has — you can send value across borders without intermediaries.
- Adoption follows an S-curve; we are still in early-to-mid adoption.
- Volatility is the price you pay for asymmetric upside in a world of currency debasement.
- **Network effect is the moat** — Bitcoin's security (hashrate) and liquidity make it increasingly difficult for competitors to displace it.
- Position sizing matters: 1-5% allocation can meaningfully improve portfolio returns without catastrophic downside risk.

### Gold

- Gold has been money for 5,000+ years and remains a reserve asset for central banks globally.
- Central banks (especially China, Russia, India, Turkey) are actively accumulating gold — this is a structural tailwind.
- Gold performs best during **negative real rate environments** (when inflation > interest rates).
- Gold miners offer leveraged upside to gold prices but come with operational/jurisdictional risk.

### Energy

- The energy transition is inflationary, not deflationary — building out renewables requires enormous amounts of hydrocarbons, copper, lithium, and other materials.
- Oil and gas companies are returning cash to shareholders via buybacks and dividends rather than increasing production — bullish for prices.
- Nuclear energy is experiencing a renaissance due to AI data center power demand and decarbonization goals.
- Uranium is in a structural deficit.

### Emerging Markets

- Emerging markets are historically cheap relative to US equities.
- Countries like India, Brazil, and parts of Southeast Asia have better demographic profiles than the US, Europe, or China.
- Dollar weakness (which Lyn expects over the long term) is a tailwind for EM assets.

## Key Mental Models

### 1. The "Milkshake" vs. "Broken Milkshake" Theory

- Brent Johnson's Dollar Milkshake Theory: The US dollar sucks up global capital due to its reserve currency status, strengthening in crises.
- Lyn's counter-framework: This works until the fiscal situation deteriorates enough that the dollar's structural advantages erode. The "milkshake machine" eventually breaks under the weight of debt.

### 2. Stock-to-Flow vs. Demand-Side Analysis

- For commodities (including Bitcoin), don't just look at stock-to-flow ratios.
- **Demand-side drivers** (global liquidity, M2 growth, credit creation) often matter more for price than supply-side dynamics alone.
- Global liquidity (measured by M2 across major economies) is the best predictor of Bitcoin and commodity prices on an 12-18 month basis.

### 3. The Cantillon Effect

- New money creation benefits those closest to the money spigot first (banks, asset owners, government contractors).
- By the time money filters down to the broader economy, prices have already risen.
- This is a primary driver of wealth inequality in financialized economies.
- **Investment implication**: Own the assets that benefit from money creation (equities, real estate, Bitcoin, gold) rather than holding depreciating cash.

### 4. Jevons Paradox (Applied to Energy)

- Making energy use more efficient doesn't reduce total energy consumption — it increases it, because efficiency makes energy cheaper, which increases demand.
- **Investment implication**: Energy demand will continue growing regardless of efficiency gains. Invest in energy production, not just efficiency.

### 5. The Triffin Dilemma

- The country that provides the world's reserve currency must run persistent trade deficits to supply the world with that currency.
- This hollows out domestic manufacturing and increases inequality.
- Over time, this creates political pressure to de-dollarize or restructure the system.
- **We are in the early stages of this restructuring now.**

### 6. Lindy Effect

- The longer something has survived, the longer it's likely to continue surviving.
- Applied to money: Gold (5,000+ years) > Fiat currencies (50 years off the gold standard) > Bitcoin (15+ years).
- Applied to businesses: Companies with 50+ year track records of dividend payments are more likely to continue than recent high-flyers.

## Common Investor Mistakes (Per Lyn Alden)

1. **Home country bias** — Over-allocating to your own country's stock market. US investors are especially guilty of this after a decade of US outperformance.

2. **Recency bias** — Assuming the recent past (low inflation, US tech dominance, strong dollar) will continue indefinitely.

3. **Confusing nominal and real returns** — Your portfolio might be "up" in dollar terms while losing purchasing power.

4. **Ignoring fiscal/monetary regime changes** — The playbook that worked in 2010-2020 (buy US growth stocks, hold long bonds) may not work in 2020-2030.

5. **Treating Bitcoin like a tech stock** — Bitcoin is a monetary asset, not a company. Valuation frameworks for equities don't apply.

6. **Underestimating commodity cycle duration** — Commodity bull markets typically last 10-15 years due to the long lead times for new production.

7. **Over-relying on backtests** — Historical backtests assume the future resembles the past. Regime changes (like the shift from monetary to fiscal dominance) break backtested strategies.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **Audit your portfolio for inflation resilience** — Ask: "If inflation averages 4-6% for the next decade, which of my holdings benefit and which suffer?"

2. **Reduce long-duration bond exposure** — Replace with short-duration Treasuries, TIPS, or I-bonds.

3. **Add commodity exposure** — Energy producers, miners, and agricultural companies are underrepresented in most portfolios.

4. **Consider a Bitcoin allocation (1-5%)** — Treat it as portfolio insurance against currency debasement with asymmetric upside.

5. **Maintain physical gold exposure** — 5-10% of portfolio as a crisis hedge and inflation hedge.

6. **Diversify internationally** — Add exposure to cheap emerging markets and commodity-exporting nations.

7. **Focus on companies with pricing power** — Businesses that can raise prices with inflation protect your purchasing power.

8. **Stay liquid** — Keep 10-20% in short-term instruments for buying opportunities during dislocations.

9. **Think in decades, not quarters** — The macro forces Lyn describes play out over 5-15 year periods. Patience is a competitive advantage.

10. **Update your framework, not just your portfolio** — The biggest risk is using a 2010s mental model in a 2020s macro environment.
